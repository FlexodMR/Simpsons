Camera

 

This document addresses the gameplay camera. Although it touches on presentation (e.g., bullet time, cut cameras for vehicle jumps, etc.) it does not cover all game presentation elements.

 

Goals

 

  • In-car camera shows:
    • the user what they need to see (down the road)
    • the car and suspension effects
    • chase vehicles
    • special events (jumps, or enemy explosions, etc.)
    • vehicle accidents (with smooth camera motion as in Road Rage)
    • use camera f/x (e.g., shake on crash, explosions, etc.)
  • The camera movement is smooth (e.g., left/right and up/down camera damping/delay)
    • Exceptions for special situations (e.g., fast spin after j-turn, smooth during accidents)
  • Clear design guidelines:
    • Overhang limits
    • How to handle passing through dynamic objects
    • Indoor driving area limitations (e.g., same as outdoor)
  • Clear, consistent, and mutually exclusive tuning values (e.g., up/down damping not affected by the speed of the car)
  • Head-2-head camera helps you see the opponent vehicle
  • On-foot camera:
    • Moves smoothly to make the character control feel smooth
    • Shows off characters but doesn’t reveal limitations in art
    • Presents the gameplay elements (e.g., conversations, and interactive world objects)
      • Camera can point at anything
    • Has a simple way of dealing with viewing problems (e.g., if a character is behind an obstacle, allow user rotation of camera)

 

Features

 

  • The core in-car camera system for driving from Road Rage will generally work again, with a few exceptions:
    • The up/down damping of camera movement would override the vertical camera placement. This camera damping had to be disabled. The camera acted as if the road between it and the car was elastic, so driving fast would cause it to drop to the ground. It should act more like it’s on a fixed stick, which is affixed to the car via suspension-like springs.
    • Keep the feature that allows a separate camera rotation speed after certain events (e.g., j-turn stops, accidents, etc.).
    • Change the camera for vehicle reversing – it should swing around to show the car from the front
    • Certain camera features should be independent of other variables (in particular the left/right and up/down rotation delays and damping)
  • The camera will smoothly pull back and up during a chase, so the chase vehicles can be seen better
    • Tuned via global parameters of a camera location relative to vehicle – not specific to vehicles
  • Head 2 head features:
    • “Target camera” when in bullet mode – camera stays in 3rd person perspective, but rotates around your vehicle to keep the opponent car as the camera target
    • Separately tunable FOV
    • Experiment with going to full-screen during bullet time (using the POV of the player who triggered it)
    • Question Is there a reasonable system for dealing with the differences between various TVs? Or do we just tune it for worst-case TVs again and let high-end TV owners suffer?
  • Camera f/x – shake on crash, explosions
  • “Cut cameras” (i.e., during jumps) will use auto-zooming to show off vehicles better
    • Investigate “camera shake” to give camera that hand-held feel
    • Cut camera on jumps stays turned on until vehicle lands (e.g., even if it’s left the camera trigger volume, as in GTA3)
    • Experiment with “bullet time” slow motion effect in cut cameras
    • Experiment with using bullet-time cut cameras in non-jump situations, including “target camera” (for example, showing your car, but rotating the camera so that you can also see a chase car blowing up)
      • Question – what are the specific situations the game could capture where this could work? Can some of these situations be pre-planned and scripted by designers?
  • On-foot camera:
    • Smooth camera movement – as if the camera is attached to the character with a rubber band; see PS2 Crash Bandicoot for an example – this kind of camera movement makes the character control feel smooth
    • Baldur’s Gate or Banjo-Tooie style when walking through the world – we tune the height and relative distance from the user. The user’s character can face the camera.
    • Colliding with a collision volume – rotate the camera angle as in Banjo-tooie.
    • Camera settings for conversations – closer, lower angle? See presentation document.
    • What about static and dynamic object camera collisions?
    • Show interactive world objects/changes:
      • As a clue – a specific walking location triggers a camera that shows you a world object that can be manipulated (e.g., a ferry)
      • As a result – when you “push the button” (e.g., enter a trigger volume) the camera shows the change, such as the ferry moving
      • See presentation document for details
    • Change POV during chase gamplay to emphasize objects doing the chasing (i.e. truckasoraus)
  • Look Back Button:
    • Used for chase gameplay.  (POV is from front of car to allow driving to still be possible)
    • Can be combined with bullet time to make it ‘quick and easy’

 

Questions

 

  • Do we need a near and far camera choice? Or just one well tuned camera location?

 

Tools

 

  • Workflow for tuning and setting up cameras:
    • Determine which camera variables can be global, and which need to be vehicle-specific
    • Tuning values should be clear and use a consistent scale (e.g., 1 to 10, or 1 to 100, or 1 to 1000 – use the same system for all values) so that anyone can tune them
    • Tuning values are mutually exclusive; they do not affect each other. For example, in Road Rage the vertical damping that overrode the vertical camera position.
    • The bumper-camera should be implied by the vehicle model, not specified explicitly.

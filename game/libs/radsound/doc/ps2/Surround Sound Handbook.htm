What types of surround sound are available for the PS2?

There are three types of surround sound:

  • Dolby Digital (digital output via SP/DIF connector)
  • DTS (digital output via SP/DIF connector)
  • Dolby ProLogic (via analogue output)

DTS Surround Sound

How can I perform DTS digital surround sound output?

DTS have a PS2 SDK available, which will mix up to 6 channels of PCM sound into a DTS compatible stream in realtime, and send it out the SP/DIF optical out connector at the back of the PS2. For more information, contact:

Dolby Surround Sound

What types of Dolby Surround Sound are available for the PS2?

There are two types of Dolby surround sound available for the PS2:

  1. Dolby Digital (also known as AC3 and 5.1)
  2. Dolby Prologic

Dolby Digital

  1. It is currently not possible to encode Dolby Digital in real time on the PS2. Processing time would be high. Also, another side effect would be that all of your SFX would need to be stored as RAW PCM data - rather than as ADPCM. This, therefore would take a lot of RAM!
  2. Playing Dolby Digital will mean that you can not play any other sound at the same time. The PlayStation 2 will set a mode which will bypass all other audio playback if you are playing a Dolby Digital bit stream. This makes sense really, as the 5.1 bit stream is, say, like a MP3 file. You can't just start adding more data to this file without having to go through the whole encoding process.
  3. I recommend using Dolby Digital for FMV and cut sequences. It does sound very good, but is not suited for in-game purposes.
  4. See the sample code SimpleSPDIF.ZIP which is available on the support web site for how to incorporate Dolby Digital playback in your project.

Dolby Pro-logic

  1. Dolby Pro-logic can work in real time.
  2. Dolby Pro-logic treats the back two speakers as mono.
  3. Real time Dolby Pro-logic takes hardly any processing time.

How is Dolby Pro-logic played back in real time?

  1. You need to play the same sample on two different SPU channels, with the same pitch/ADSR settings.
  2. A small delay is required between keying on the first and second SPU channel. Just by setting up the pitch/ADSR1/ADSR2/SPU Address is enough of a delay, so it is not really a problem.
  3. Then, you need to set the volumes of these two channels like this:

        SPUChannel1_Volume = Front_Left, Front_Right (as normal)
        SPUChannel2_Volume = Back_Volume, -BackVolume (sets the volume for the rear speakers)

    Note that because the top bit of the SPU2 Volume registers is used for other purposes, such as automatic volume fade up/down etc. You need really to do this:

        SPUChannel2_Volume = Back_Volume, 0x7fff - BackVolume

    And that's it!

Why can't I mix these two channel volumes together and only use one channel?

You can't mix these two channel volumes together and then use only one SPU channel due to the fact that both channels will be actually playing slightly different sound data (due to the delay between the keying on for the second channel)

Is it possible with only one channel?

Yes, but you will have a few problems:
  1. You will hear the sound flip from back/left to back/right if the source of the sound moves across the centre of the screen.
  2. You can't play the SFX at full volume (using one channel involves a channel volume "bleeding" through to the back speakers (ie. volume = 0x2000,-0x4000).
  3. So its only really useful for single spot FX which are far away. Like something that will ALWAYS only require one channel and that won't move around the playing area too much, which would cause the sound to flip from the front left/right speakers. Also, being far away, it would not require its volume level to be high anyway.
  4. Basically, I recommend using two channels if possible, so you never have to deal with these problems anyway.
